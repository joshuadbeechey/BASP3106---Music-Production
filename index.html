BASP 3106 - Music Production

Week 1 Module

Module title

Found Sound as Instrument: Capturing, Transforming, and Composing in Ableton Live

Module overview

In this module, you will explore how everyday sound can become musical material. Rather than starting with traditional instruments, you will begin with recorded sounds from the world around you and learn how to shape them into rhythm, texture, harmony, and form inside Ableton Live.

You will learn how to record usable sound, organise and edit audio, apply transformation techniques, and build a short 3 - 4 minute composition from non-musical source material. This module also introduces the expectations for Main Evidence 1: Composition from Found Sound.

Why this matters

Found sound composition expands your understanding of what music can be made from. It encourages close listening, creative experimentation, and technical control. By working with recorded sound, you will develop:

  • Critical listening skills
  • Recording technique
  • Audio editing and session organisation skills
  • Creative sound design approaches
  • A stronger understanding of how musical ideas can emerge from texture, rhythm, and timbre

Learning outcomes

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

  • Explain the creative brief for Main Evidence 1: Composition from Found Sound
  • Record field audio with appropriate gain staging and minimal unwanted noise
  • Import, organise, and label audio in an Ableton Live session
  • Apply fundamental editing processes such as trimming, fades, clip gain, and consolidation
  • Use warping and time-stretching with purpose
  • Transform raw recordings using slicing, resampling, and effects processing
  • Build a short musical sketch from found-sound-derived material

1. Found sound

Found sound refers to audio collected from everyday life rather than recorded from traditional musical instruments. These sounds may include footsteps, doors, traffic, machines, kitchen utensils, voices, wind, room ambience, or any other environmental source.

The key idea is that a sound does not need to begin as “musical” in order to become part of a musical composition.

Key concept summary

Found sound is everyday audio used as creative source material. The musical value comes from how the sound is selected, shaped, and organised, not from whether it began as an instrument.

2. Capture

Capturing sound is your first creative step. Good recordings make later editing and transformation much easier.

When recording, focus on:

  • Recording at safe levels without clipping
  • Reducing unwanted background noise where possible
  • Changing microphone distance for variation
  • Recording multiple versions of the same sound
  • Capturing contrasting textures such as short hits, long drones, scrapes, impacts, and ambience

Key concept summary

Strong recordings create stronger outcomes. Clean gain staging, variation, and a wide range of textures give you better material to edit, transform, and compose with later.

3. Transform

Transformation is the process of changing recorded sound into something new. We will make use of Simpler, Sampler, Drum Sampler, and Wavetable in the creation of instruments. This is where sound begins to move from documentation to composition.

Common transformation techniques include:

  • Trimming to isolate the most useful part of a sound
  • Fades to remove clicks and smooth transitions
  • Clip gain to balance levels
  • Consolidation to create clean, usable audio files
  • Warping to change timing and rhythm
  • Time-stretching to exaggerate or reshape texture
  • Slicing to break audio into playable fragments
  • Resampling to record processed sound and reuse it as new source material
  • Effects processing such as EQ, saturation, compression, delay, and reverb

Key concept summary

Transformation turns raw recordings into musical material. Editing, warping, slicing, resampling, and effects allow one sound to take on new rhythmic, tonal, or textural roles.

4. Compose

Composition means organising transformed sounds into a meaningful musical idea. A found-sound composition can still contain familiar musical roles, even when you use no traditional instruments.

Think about how your found sounds can function as:

  • Rhythm through repeated impacts, clicks, taps, or pulses
  • Texture through ambience, noise, layering, and movement
  • Harmony or pitch material through tuned samples, stretched tones, or resonant sounds
  • Lead material through distinctive foreground sounds that attract attention

Key concept summary

Composition is the process of giving transformed sounds musical purpose. Found sounds can still act as rhythm, harmony, texture, and lead material when arranged with intention.

5. Session organisation

A clear session structure will help you work efficiently and make better creative decisions. In Ableton Live, your recordings should be arranged and labelled clearly so that raw recordings, edited materials, instruments, and resampled sounds are easy to find.

Suggested track groups:

  • RAW
  • EDIT
  • INSTRUMENTS
  • RESAMPLE

Key concept summary

Good session organisation supports creativity. Clear naming, grouping, and separation of materials make it easier to work quickly, stay focused, and manage complex ideas.

Part A. Listening and context

Begin by listening to examples of found-sound composition. As you listen, identify:

  • Which sounds seem recognisable
  • Which sounds have been transformed
  • How rhythm, texture, and movement are created
  • What makes the result feel musical rather than purely documentary

This will help you understand that musicality can come from structure, repetition, contrast, and intent, not only from melody or traditional instruments.

Key concept summary

Listening builds awareness of how found sound becomes music. Musicality often comes from structure, repetition, contrast, and intention rather than from conventional melody alone.

Part B. Understanding the brief

Review the expectations for Main Evidence 1. The main requirement is that your composition must be derived primarily from found sound.

This means you should:

  • Use recorded real-world audio as your main source material
  • Show evidence of transformation and creative development
  • Avoid relying on conventional instruments as the primary musical content

Key concept summary

The brief requires found sound to be the main source of your composition. Success depends on clear transformation, creative development, and limited reliance on traditional instruments.

Part C. Field recording fundamentals

Before you begin composing, collect a variety of recordings. Think like a sound collector and record multiple textures and perspectives.

A useful starting exercise is to record 5 contrasting sounds:

  • A short impact
  • A scrape or friction sound
  • A sustained drone
  • An ambient background sound
  • A voice-like or expressive sound

Key concept summary

Field recording is about gathering a useful and varied source library. Recording contrasting sounds gives you a broader sample palette and more compositional possibilities.

Part D. Importing and preparing audio

Once your recordings are in Ableton Live, organise your session carefully.

This stage includes:

  • Importing files into the project
  • Naming clips clearly
  • Colour coding related sounds
  • Sorting recordings into track groups
  • Adding notes where useful

You should then edit your recordings into a smaller, more deliberate sample palette.

Key concept summary

Importing and preparation turn raw files into a workable palette. Clear naming, colour coding, grouping, and selection help you move from collecting sounds to using them creatively.

Part E. Editing and cleaning source material

Editing is not just technical correction. It is also the beginning of composition.

You should:

  • Trim silence or unwanted handling noise
  • Add fades to avoid clicks
  • Adjust clip gain for consistency
  • Consolidate clips into clean, reusable files
  • Select the strongest 10 to 15 sounds for your sample palette

Key concept summary

Editing improves both clarity and creativity. Cleaning and selecting your best sounds helps you build a focused sample palette that is ready for musical use.

Part F. Warping and timing

Warping allows you to reshape the timing of sound. This is especially important when turning long recordings or irregular events into loops and rhythmic structures.

Explore how different warp modes affect sound quality and musical feel:

  • Beats for percussive and rhythmic material
  • Tones for more tonal content
  • Texture for grainy or atmospheric reshaping
  • Complex for fuller sounds that need broader preservation

Key concept summary

Warping gives you rhythmic control over recorded sound. Choosing the right warp mode helps preserve the qualities you need while adapting the audio for musical timing.

Part G. Transformation and resampling

At this stage, you will move further into sound design. A single source recording can become many different musical elements through repeated processing.

A recommended workflow is:

  1. Choose one source sound
  1. Process it with EQ, saturation, compression, delay, or reverb
  1. Resample the result
  1. Process the new version again
  1. Create at least 3 distinct variations from the same source

This process shows you how a simple everyday sound can become rhythm, pad, bass, or lead material.

Key concept summary

Resampling expands the creative potential of a single sound. Repeated processing can generate multiple musical roles from one source and deepen your sound design process.

Part H. Building the composition

You should now begin constructing a short sketch of approximately 60 to 90 seconds.

Your sketch should include:

  • A rhythmic element
  • A bass layer or harmonic bed
  • A lead or focal texture
  • Movement created through automation or modulation

At this stage, think about contrast, repetition, pacing, and balance.

Key concept summary

A strong sketch combines core musical functions with clear movement and contrast. Even a short composition should show structure, balance, and purposeful development.

Homework task

Activity Brief:

Throughout the semester, you will submit an Ableton Live session template that you will use for all assignments. This template will serve as the foundation for your creative work and help ensure a consistent and efficient workflow. Your template should be well organised, include commonly used instruments and effects, and be customised to your workflow preferences. Refer to the criteria below for details on what your template should include.

CriteriaDescription
OrganizationThe session is logically organised, with tracks grouped and named appropriately.
Basic SetupIncludes basic setup elements such as a master track with a limiter, audio and MIDI tracks, and buses.
Commonly Used DevicesIncludes commonly used instruments (for example, synthesizers and samplers) and effects (for example, EQ and reverb).
CustomizationCustomised to your workflow preferences, including personalised default settings for devices and effects.
Efficiency FeaturesIncludes features that enhance workflow efficiency, such as key mappings, MIDI mappings, and track presets.
DocumentationIncludes documentation or notes within the template that explain the purpose and usage of different elements.

Key concept summary

The homework task focuses on building a reusable Ableton Live template. The goal is to support consistency, organisation, and efficiency across all assignments during the semester.

Key terms

  • Found sound: Everyday recorded audio used as source material for composition
  • Field recording: The act of recording sound outside a controlled studio environment
  • Warping: Adjusting the timing of audio in Ableton Live
  • Time-stretching: Changing the duration of sound without necessarily changing pitch
  • Resampling: Recording processed audio into a new file for further use
  • Sample palette: A selected set of edited audio clips prepared for composition
  • Texture: The surface quality or density of sound in a composition
  • Timbre: The character or tone colour of a sound
